Tees Valley Mayor's Green Deal Faces Corruption Probe

In a blow to Tees Valley's ambitious green ambitions, an October 14 inquiry has exposed bidding irregularities in Mayor Ben Houchen's £1bn green energy project. Launched in 2023 to transform the region into a net-zero hub via offshore wind and hydrogen tech, the initiative now risks stalling amid allegations of favoritism in contract awards. Yet, this crisis presents a winnable opportunity: implementing robust transparency reforms could safeguard public funds and accelerate sustainable growth, ensuring Tees Valley—and neighboring Northumbria—reap long-term economic dividends.

The Inquiry's Revelations

The probe, initiated by the National Audit Office (NAO) on October 14, 2025, scrutinized procurement processes for the project's core £500m phase, focusing on contracts for turbine manufacturing and site development. Verifiable findings, released October 16, highlight "non-competitive bidding practices" that bypassed standard EU-derived procurement rules, potentially inflating costs by 15-20% based on comparable UK projects like Scotland's Moray East wind farm, which adhered to strict tenders and saved £200m.

Mayor Houchen defended the deals in a October 15 press conference, stating, "We prioritized speed to secure jobs and investment in a post-Brexit landscape." However, NAO interim director Sarah Wilkins countered: "While urgency is understandable, shortcuts erode public trust and expose taxpayers to undue risk." Implications are stark: the £1bn pot, partly funded by UK Infrastructure Bank loans, faces a funding freeze until audits conclude, delaying 5,000 projected jobs and £300m in regional GDP uplift by 2027, per Tees Valley Combined Authority estimates.

Political Weaknesses Undermining Progress

Houchen's Conservative leadership has leaned heavily on private-sector partnerships, a tactic that boosted Tees Valley's post-steel recovery with £1.2bn in foreign direct investment since 2017. But the probe reveals a political blind spot: over-reliance on opaque "fast-track" approvals, echoing national scandals like the PPE contracts during COVID-19, which cost £4.8bn in waste according to a 2023 Public Accounts Committee report.

This approach weakens cross-party support essential for devolution deals. Labour's Tees Valley opposition, led by councillor Chris McEwan, has called for Houchen's resignation, arguing it distracts from core issues. Critique: Such polarization stalls bipartisan reforms, like mandatory independent oversight boards, which have succeeded in Greater Manchester's £1.5bn clean energy fund, fostering 8,000 jobs without controversy. To win regionally, politicians must pivot to collaborative governance, integrating Northumbrian stakeholders for broader Northeast synergy and averting isolated failures.

Business Vulnerabilities and Smarter Alternatives

From a business lens, the irregularities signal deeper risks in supply chain integrity. Key contractors, including a Siemens-led consortium, benefited from sole-source bids, raising antitrust flags under the Competition Act 1998. Data from the Office for National Statistics shows similar green projects with transparent tenders yield 12% higher investor confidence and 25% faster ROI, as seen in Humber region's £800m hydrogen initiative, which attracted £500m private capital post-reform.

Houchen's model, while injecting urgency, overlooks diversified bidding that could cut costs and innovate—e.g., open RFPs inviting Northumbrian firms like Newcastle's offshore specialists, potentially adding £150m in local value chains. Alternatives abound: Adopt blockchain-led procurement, trialed in Wales' renewable sector, reducing fraud by 30% per PwC audits. Businesses in Tees Valley should lobby for these, framing them as efficiency gains that protect reputations and unlock ESG funding, vital as global green investments hit £2.5tn in 2025 per BloombergNEF.

Rebuilding Trust Through Reform

The probe underscores a need for proactive fixes: Establish a Tees Valley Transparency Commission with NAO oversight, mandating public bid disclosures and third-party audits. This mirrors successful models in the West Midlands, where reforms post-2022 inquiry boosted project delivery by 40% and public approval to 65%, per Ipsos polling. By prioritizing these, Houchen can transform scandal into strength, aligning with Northumbria's devolution push for shared green infrastructure.

In conclusion, actionable next steps are clear: Houchen should commission an independent review by October 31, 2025, integrating opposition input and piloting open tenders for the next £200m tranche. Regional businesses must engage via the Tees Valley Business Board, advocating data-driven procurement. These moves not only mitigate risks but position Tees Valley as a Northeast green leader, driving inclusive prosperity. With swift action, this probe becomes a catalyst for resilient, transparent growth.
