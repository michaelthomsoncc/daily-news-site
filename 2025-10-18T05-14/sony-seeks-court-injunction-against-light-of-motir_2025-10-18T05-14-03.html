Sony Seeks Court Injunction Against Light of Motiram

From the Gaming Releases & Updates section – Straight facts, no filter.

Imagine your favourite open-world adventure like Horizon getting copied so closely that the original makers hit the courts to stop it. That's the drama unfolding right now with Sony pushing hard for an injunction against Tencent's game, Light of Motiram. On October 17, 2025, Sony fired back in a US court, demanding a halt to the game's promotion and release over claims it's a blatant rip-off of their Horizon series. This escalates a lawsuit started back in July, shaking up the global gaming scene as fans worldwide, including in the UK, wait to see if their next big play gets blocked.

The Lawsuit Basics: What Started This Fight?

Sony Interactive Entertainment sued Tencent in July 2025, accusing the Chinese giant of copyright infringement. They called Light of Motiram a "slavish clone" of Horizon Zero Dawn and Horizon Forbidden West. Both are post-apocalyptic survival games with robot beasts and bow-wielding heroes in vast worlds. Sony claims Tencent copied core ideas, characters, and even art styles too closely, hurting their IP rights. Tencent, who owns studios like Lightspeed and Moon Studios behind the game, denied it and tried to dismiss the case. But on October 17, Sony hit back, asking the court to reject that move and push forward.

Sony's Big Push: Demanding an Injunction Now

In fresh court filings from October 17, Sony sought a preliminary injunction to stop Light of Motiram from any pre-release hype or launch. They blasted Tencent's response as "nonsense" and accused them of playing a "shell game" by shifting blame between subsidiaries. Sony insists the "damage is done" – meaning even if changes happen now, the copycat harm to Horizon's uniqueness is permanent. This could freeze Tencent's plans, delaying a game teased for PC and possibly consoles, affecting developers and players eager for fresh survival adventures.

Tencent's Side: Ready to Change, But Is It Enough?

Tencent argues the similarities are just genre tropes common in open-world games, not direct theft. They've said they're willing to tweak Light of Motiram to avoid issues, like altering designs or mechanics. But Sony isn't buying it, pointing out the game's trailers already mimic Horizon's robotic enemies and exploration vibes. Court docs from October 17 highlight how Tencent's motion to dismiss ignores key evidence of copying, from character models to world-building. This back-and-forth keeps the case alive in California's federal court, with no quick resolution in sight.

Global Ripples: How This Hits UK Gamers

For UK players grinding daily on PS5 or PC, this feud means uncertainty around Light of Motiram's availability. If the injunction sticks, it could block Steam or other platforms from promoting it here, just as Horizon fans crave more Aloy-like epics. Sony's win might protect creative originals, boosting UK studios inspired by safe IP. But a loss could flood markets with clones, diluting what makes games special. Updates from gaming news on October 17, like those buzzing on X, show the community split – some cheer Sony's stand, others worry about stifling competition in the £7 billion UK industry.

Keep eyes on this: Next court hearing could drop soon, deciding if Light of Motiram sees light or stays in the dark. For young gamers, it's a reminder that behind epic quests, real battles over ideas shape what we play next.
