Food Scene Buzz: Vegan Festival Takes Over Exhibition Park

From the Sustainability and Eco Efforts section

In the heart of Newcastle upon Tyne, Exhibition Park transformed into a vibrant hub of green cuisine and conscious living on October 17, 2025, as the inaugural Newcastle Vegan Festival kicked off under crisp autumn skies. Drawing hundreds of eco-conscious visitors from across the North East, the event celebrated plant-based innovation with an array of local vendors, insightful talks, and a palpable sense of community. This debut marks a milestone in the city's evolving food landscape, where ethical dining is no longer a niche but a noticeable trend.

A Feast of Plant-Based Delights

The festival's core attraction lay in its diverse lineup of food stalls, where local vendors showcased the best of vegan creativity. Over 30 exhibitors filled the park's expansive lawns, offering everything from smoky jackfruit tacos by Tyneside Tacos, a Geordie favorite known for its fusion flavors, to decadent chocolate truffles from Northumbrian Nibbles, crafted with ethically sourced cacao. Attendees raved about the "seitan kebabs that rival any traditional barbecue," as one visitor shared while sampling from Veggie Vibes, a Newcastle-based stall specializing in Middle Eastern-inspired dishes.

Organizers reported a strong emphasis on regional ingredients, with vendors like Quayside Greens highlighting foraged mushrooms and heirloom vegetables from nearby Northumberland farms. The event's zero-waste policy ensured all packaging was compostable, aligning with the festival's sustainability ethos. By midday, queues snaked through the park, a testament to the growing demand for plant-based options in a city traditionally famed for its hearty, meat-centric meals.

Educational Sessions on Sustainable Living

Beyond the bites, the festival delved into the why behind the what through a series of talks and workshops held in a central marquee. Experts from organizations like the Plant Based Treaty and local group North East Animal Rights led discussions on sustainable farming practices. Dr. Elena Hargrove, a lecturer in environmental science at Newcastle University, opened the session with data on how plant-based diets could reduce the UK's carbon footprint by up to 30%, citing recent 2025 reports from the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change.

"Switching to veganism isn't just about food; it's a powerful step toward regenerative agriculture," Hargrove emphasized, pointing to initiatives in the region that promote crop rotation and soil health. Workshops on cruelty-free cosmetics and ethical fashion complemented the lineup, with hands-on demos teaching attendees how to make plant-based skincare at home. These sessions, free with festival entry, attracted families and students alike, fostering deeper engagement with the movement.

Vibrant Atmosphere and Community Spirit

Exhibition Park buzzed with energy as live music from local vegan-friendly bands like Green Echoes provided a soundtrack to the day's festivities. Yoga sessions in the morning drew early birds, while children's areas featured plant-powered crafts and storytelling about animal welfare. The crowd was diverse, spanning young professionals snapping photos for social media to retirees exploring new dietary paths, all united by a shared commitment to compassion and the planet.

Festival-goers like Sarah Jenkins, a 28-year-old from Gateshead, described the event as "a breath of fresh air in a city that's finally catching up with global trends." With sunny intervals and temperatures hovering around 12°C, the park's historic setting—once home to the 19th-century Great North Exhibition—added a layer of charm. Security was unobtrusive, and organizers noted smooth operations despite the turnout exceeding initial estimates of 500 visitors.

Local Vendors and Economic Boost

For Newcastle's burgeoning vegan scene, the festival served as a launchpad for small businesses. Vendors reported brisk sales, with Bean There, a coffee roaster using oat milk alternatives, selling out their signature lattes by early afternoon. The event's timing aligns with a 2025 surge in UK veganism, where surveys from Vegan Society indicate 1.5 million vegans nationwide, up 15% from the previous year, driven by health, environmental, and ethical concerns.

Local leaders welcomed the initiative. Councillor Maria Fernandes of Newcastle City Council stated, "Events like this not only support our creative entrepreneurs but also position Newcastle as a forward-thinking destination for sustainable tourism." The festival's footprint included partnerships with the city's public transport system, encouraging low-emission travel and tying into broader regional goals for net-zero by 2030.

Broader Implications for Newcastle's Food Culture

The debut of the Newcastle Vegan Festival underscores a shift in the city's culinary identity. Once synonymous with pease pudding and stottie cakes, Newcastle now boasts over 50 fully vegan eateries, from the upscale Plantation House in the city center to casual spots like Greens & Grains in Jesmond. This growth mirrors national patterns, with 2025 data from Mintel showing plant-based products capturing 12% of the UK grocery market.

Challenges remain, such as accessibility for lower-income households, but events like this highlight inclusive options, with many stalls offering affordable £5 meals. The festival also spotlighted intersectional issues, including how veganism intersects with cultural traditions in diverse communities across Tyneside.

As the sun set on Exhibition Park, the Newcastle Vegan Festival wrapped its first day with promises of return visits and an expanded 2026 edition. With attendee feedback overwhelmingly positive and plans already underway for more regional collaborations, the event signals a lasting embrace of ethical eating in the Toon. For those who missed it, recordings of talks will be available online via the festival's website, ensuring the conversation—and the flavors—linger on.
