Sanctions Reloaded: What the UN Did

The Security Council extended Haiti's sanctions regime for another year, no votes against. This includes a travel ban, asset freeze, and arms embargo—all tools to choke off gang funding and firepower. The resolution, drafted by Panama and the US, renews the Haiti Panel of Experts' mandate too. Their job? Track violations and report back, like intel scouts in a strategy game. UN News reports the gangs are still wreaking havoc, displacing thousands and blocking aid, making everyday survival a high-stakes quest.

Targeting the Players: New Sanctions Hit Hard

Spotlight on two key figures: a former police officer and a gang leader, both slapped with US and UN sanctions over criminal ties. The UN's move blocks their assets and travel, aiming to dismantle networks fueling the violence. Al Jazeera notes the arms embargo, in place since 2022, got another extension— no weapons flowing in to amp up the gangs' arsenal. In Haiti, this means ports and roads stay choke points for illegal arms, but experts say enforcement is key to leveling the playing field for civilians.

Global Pushback: Tensions in the Talks

Negotiations weren't smooth. China and Russia clashed with Western nations over mentions of the Gang Suppression Force (GSF), a Kenyan-led mission backed by the UN. The US Mission to the UN stressed these sanctions are "key components of a broader international effort to promote peace and stability in Haiti—and within the region." Real-World Grind: Impacts on Haiti

Gangs control 80% of Port-au-Prince, per recent reports, turning schools into battlegrounds and hospitals into targets. Over 5,600 killed or injured this year alone, with kids caught in the crossfire—think no safe respawn. The sanctions aim to starve the violence, but UN voices warn without more boots on the ground, like expanding the GSF, the crisis drags on.
